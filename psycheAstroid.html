Psyche Astroid Sample Return

Design

Psyche is a metal-rich asteroid, currently orbiting the Sun between Mars and Jupiter. Arizona State University oversees the missions to explore the asteroid. The first mission is in progress and launched in October of 2023 to explore the origin of the asteroid. The second mission will return samples of the asteroid to Earth in 2029. Obtaining authentic samples will allow researchers to gain a better understanding of the surfaces of the asteroid. A spacecraft will hold a sealing system, holding samples after extraction. Our sealing system could be utilized to seal and protect samples from contamination. The goal of our project is to design a sealing system for collected samples from the Psyche asteroid. The final design consists of a fully automated sealing system that maintains the integrity of samples from the asteroid. Most of the characteristics of Psyche are only hypothesized, such as material and surface texture. We consider such associated technical challenges with our design. Challenges for this project include simulating the environment- controlled mission’s conditions within the spacecraft, including a wide range of temperatures and a low acceleration of gravity. The system aims to function under such extreme conditions in addition to remaining cost effective and versatile.

Plug Design

Two pieces make up the body of the plug. The plug is design to be placed into a round vial, which holds the same. The screw threads the pieces together and compresses the O-ring once screwed in. It is important to note the undercuts along the circumference of the top of the plug. The grippers hold onto the plug by these undercuts. The spring serves to provide uniform force retention and additional support when travelling down the vial, while also cleaning the walls of the vial. The plug allows for the sample to be safely returned to Earth, while also protecting it.

Automation

The linear actuator rod moves the gripper assembly down. The stepper motor controls the gears. The gears (pinions) and racks act to form a rack and pinion concept, moving the lowering attachment. The stepper motor controls the rotation of the gears. The gears allow the racks to open and close horizontally. As the racks open, the lowering attachment can directly interact with the plug. The lowering attachment holds and pushes down the plug. The lowering attachment interacts via the undercuts inside the plug to push the plug against the sample within the vial. When the racks and lowering attachment are closed, the attachment can fit into the plug. The lowering attachment expands against the undercuts of the top portion of the plug. The attachment then pushes the plug into the vial and further pushes the plug down the vial to meet the sample. Once the plug reaches the sample, the racks close again and retract the lowering attachment upward, leaving the plug in the vial. For the screwing process, the lowering attachment fully expands to the end of the racks, moving out of the way for the screwing attachment to come in. A stepper motor in the sealing station rotates the screwing attachment. The screwing attachment rotates the screw, ultimately compressing the O-ring and create the seal.

To fully automate the sealing process, the sealing station detects the orientation of the screw head. Based on our design, the screw is inside the vial, without being screwed down all the way before the space craft is launched into space. While the space craft travels up, the screw could rotate to an unknown position and change orientation. Once the detection of the screw head occurs, the screwing attachment’s hex bit matches with the alignment of the screw head and screws downward. We created an algorithm to detect the orientation of the screw using color detection. The algorithm relies on two green dots that are added to the screw head.
